## **ABSTRACT** 

Using newly digitised colonial medical time-series data, this study makes a first attempt to provide an overview of the development of Western healthcare in Northern Rhodesia. The data is used to elaborate on the extent of the Christian missionary medical outreach to the African populace and the involvement of the African medical workers in establishing and expanding the Western healthcare system in Northern Rhodesia. The article details the colonial government’s contribution to fighting epidemics while accentuating how missionaries and trained Africans aided the colonial government. The findings show that with the help of indigenous auxiliaries, missionaries extended Western medicine to the African population. Missionary hospitals attended to a greater proportion of the local population than the African colonial government hospitals. With different agendas at times, the state cooperated with missionaries to provide healthcare to most of the local people in rural areas. With assistance from the missionaries and trained Africans, the British colonial government played a significant role in fighting outbreaks. However, interventions were sometimes racially segregative, especially in urban areas.

Missionaries were the main healthcare providers in form and quantity in many African colonial states for most of the colonial era (Doyle, Meier zu Selhausen & Weisdorf 2020; Kalusa 2007, 2014a; Vaughan 1991, 1994). For example, in Ghana, health lagged in the early twentieth century; it was not until the 1920s, when the colonial government joined forces with the missionaries, that healthcare became more robust (Walker 2022). Faith-based organisations still provide significant healthcare in Africa (Olivier, Tsimpo, Gemignani et al. 2015). However, the scope of this article does not allow for a longterm analysis of healthcare in Zambia; the study is confined to the colonial period. 

There was a strong contrast between the care governments and missionary societies provided in Africa during colonialism, with the former focusing on prevention and the latter on curative healthcare (Doyle et al. 2020; Hokkanen 2019). However, the generalisability of this dichotomy across the rest of Africa remains uncertain due to limited research in this area. The interaction between the state and missionaries varied across Africa, necessitating further investigation to unravel the complexities of their interactions in different regions. For instance, some literature suggests colonial governments viewed missionaries with suspicion, fearing they undermined colonial authority; thus, in such instances, there was little cooperation between the state and missionaries. However, other studies highlight extensive cooperation between colonial governments and missionaries in healthcare, as seen in Zimbabwe and Uganda (Vongsathorn 2012). In the case of Northern Rhodesia, it is evident that the colonial government recognised the significant advancements missionaries made in delivering curative healthcare, particularly in rural areas where most of the population resided. Given the greater need for infrastructure investment in curative care and the limited funding available to the colonial government during the early years of Northern Rhodesia’s administration, their focus primarily remained on preventative healthcare. Notably, preventative care experienced gradual development, but it took on a more robust form during World War II when the demand for copper increased, providing the government with additional revenue to allocate toward their medical campaigns. 

Further, the most sustained encounter Africans had with ‘Western’ biomedicine was through the missionaries, who subsequently developed significant trust with local communities. As colonial government prevention campaigns took form in rural areas, missionaries and African assistants facilitated these efforts on behalf of the government (Gelfand 1961; Hokkanen 2019). However, the pioneering role of medical missionaries in establishing Western healthcare in colonial Africa is unparalleled (Hardiman 2006). It is crucial to highlight the significant contributions of African auxiliaries in establishing and disseminating Western healthcare. The Euro-centric colonial medical discourse has often underemphasised the role of indigenous health workers in spreading Western biomedical practices. Given the limited number of missionaries and the vast territories to be covered, missionaries recognised the necessity of training ‘African evangelists’ to aid in their mission (Porter 2004). 

Thus, two strands of literature emerge when studying healthcare in Northern Rhodesia: colonial and post-independence. Colonial discourse often promoted the colonial powers’

## **Development of missionary and colonial healthcare** 

This study primarily concentrates on comparing the healthcare outreach efforts of African state hospitals and missionary hospitals, as their activities and impact are more extensively documented and offer a clearer understanding of the broader healthcare landscape in the context of Northern Rhodesia. 

In Northern Rhodesia, similar to other pre-colonial African societies, traditional healers played a significant role in the administration of medical care (Burke-Gaffney 1968). Indigenous people had established their own traditional public healthcare systems, utilising knowledge passed down through generations (Jennings 2008; Waite 1992). Many illnesses within African communities were treated within households, drawing upon the traditional medicinal knowledge encompassing the use of leaves, saps, herbs, and roots (Waite 1992). It is important to note that not all illnesses were attributed to spiritual causes, contrary to Western beliefs. While certain illnesses were associated with spiritual origins, traditional healers would perform ancestral rites. Individuals would sacrifice to appease ancestral spirits if neglect of these rites were believed to cause a particular disease (Ifeanyi 1992; Waite 1992). 

During the initial phases of missionary establishment in Africa, the missionaries sought to ‘civilise’ and ‘save the souls’ of Africans, resulting in attempts to supplant African traditional medical practices that missionaries considered heathen (Aguwa 2007; Vaughan 1991). Conversion to Christianity required Africans to abandon various aspects of their indigenous religions and cultures (ibid). However, the missionaries’ efforts to eradicate indigenous African medical practices proved unsuccessful. Instead, African missionarytrained medical auxiliaries assimilated elements of missionary medicine into their local context, leading to the perception of evangelical medicine as a variant of their indigenous medical culture (Kalusa 2007). 

The establishment of the Western-style healthcare system in Northern Rhodesia can be attributed to the missionaries, with the London Mission Society (LMS) being the first mission society to pioneer medical work in the region. They established the Wattle and Daub dispensary at Niamkolo in 1891 (Rotberg 1965). Missionaries received basic medical training during their seminary education, equipping them with knowledge in wound care, draining purulent sores, and attending to patients with fever (Crozier

It took time for Africans to become accustomed to Western healing methods, but as the efficacy of Western medicine in treating certain illnesses became evident, an increasing number of Africans started seeking treatment at missionary clinics (Patterson 1981; Rotberg 1965). For instance, Dr Brien, a Pentecostal missionary in Katerere, Zimbabwe, demonstrated the effectiveness of combining prayer with medicine (Maxwell 1997). This led those with dental, sight and hearing problems to seek treatment at the missionary clinic. 

In response to the increasing number of clinic attendances, missionaries recognised their limitations in treating various tropical diseases due to a lack of appropriate medical expertise (Rotberg 1965). Consequently, around 1900, missionaries began appealing to their mother churches to send more trained medical missionary doctors and financial support to expand the scope of their medical services (ibid). The missionaries highlighted that providing comprehensive medical care would facilitate their evangelism, as many sought treatment at their basic medical facilities (Gann 1968). Despite the missionaries’ appeals, the assistance from sending churches was sometimes insufficient (Rotberg 1965). In an appeal to the Paris Evangelical Mission for medical doctors in 1894, Francis Coillard wrote: 

_I hope you will not lose sight of the appeal we are making for two medical missionaries. Is it that among young Christian men who have chosen this profession, not a single one has yet offered himself for the Zambesi? Is it because we are so often ill? But not a single doctor has yet heard the call of God?_ (Gelfand 1961) 

Certain missionary societies received more financial support from their sending churches, leading many missionaries to engage in commercial activities to reduce their dependency on these churches (Snelson 1974). During the period of British South African Company (BSAC) rule in Northern Rhodesia from 1890 to 1924, the Company provided limited support for missionary medical work (Gelfand 1961). However, by 1899, faced with challenges in delivering medical services to the small European population, the Company turned to missionaries for assistance (ibid). An offer was made to the LMS of £200 to provide medical services to an estimated population of approximately 150 Europeans residing in Kawimbe and Niamkolo. Despite the willingness of local missionaries to help the Company, the LMS Secretary in London declined the offer, fearing that accepting payments for attending to the European population would divert their focus from the primary mission of proselytising the Africans (Gelfand 1961). In 1901, the LMS changed its stance and agreed to receive subsidies from the BSAC, eventually receiving £50 in aid as recognition for their medical services in the Tanganyika plateau. 

It is important to emphasise that, despite the promise made in the Lochner concession for providing public services such as education and healthcare, the British South African Company failed to fulfil this commitment. The interests of the growing white settler population rather than the well-being of the African population drove the limited investments in medical services (Gann 1968). In 1902, the Company appointed doctors at their main administrative centres in North-Eastern Rhodesia, namely Abercorn and Fort Jameson (ibid). While these doctors were primarily responsible for attending to the Company’s staff and the European settlers, to some extent, they also provided healthcare services to the local African population and were involved in disease control and prevention efforts in the regions where they were stationed (Gelfand 1961). In 1906, the Company

However, despite these efforts, the medical services remained underdeveloped and were insufficient to meet the needs of the estimated local population of 820 000 (ibid). 

Consequently, the British South African Company continued to rely on medical missionaries to provide curative care to the African population. Throughout their rule, the BSAC primarily focused on preventive healthcare in Northern Rhodesia. For instance, during a rat plague outbreak in the Kambombo area of North-Eastern Rhodesia in 1917, the Company implemented measures to control and prevent the spread of the disease (Gelfand 1961). These measures included offering a monetary reward to the public for each rat killed and closing the Mzimba-Rhodesian border while deploying Askaris to regulate movement in the region (ibid). The Company’s preventive efforts effectively halted the spread of the rat plague, with no reported cases by the end of 1918 (ibid).

The Company developed European hospitals only at the main administrative centres (Gelfand 1961). ## **Missionary and colonial healthcare outreach** 

This section discusses the role of missionaries in healthcare provision in Northern Rhodesia, emphasising their crucial contributions to curative and preventative medicine, particularly in the context of the colonial state’s limited resources and infrastructure. 

The primary motivation of missionaries was to save the souls of the African population, as they strongly believed that by demonstrating their ability to cure illnesses, they would be more successful in spreading their religious message (Feierman 1985; Hardiman 2006; Vaughan 1991). Missionaries aimed to transform local ideologies and bring about a change in mentality among their patients, attributing diseases to certain traditional practices and seeking to conquer them through the propagation of Christian morals (Vaughan 1991). While state medicine focused on disease prevention through vaccination campaigns and sanitation improvements, missionary medicine was primarily concerned with healing and curative care (Manton 2006; Shankar 2006; Vaughan 1991).

However, it is important to note that missionaries also played a significant role in vaccination campaigns and provided services like maternity care and child welfare, which can be considered forms of preventative medicine (Jennings 2008). Limited resources hindered the curative outreach efforts of the colonial state, a pattern observed not only in Northern Rhodesia but also in other colonial territories such as Tanganyika and Malawi (Hokkanen 2019; Jennings 2008; Tilley 2016). A lack of funding and trained personnel constrained the state’s medical services, making the missionaries crucial in meeting the healthcare needs of the rural African population (Tilley 2016). 

Over time, the missionaries managed to gain the trust of the local communities, a crucial factor in their success as they often relied on the trust, they had fostered to conduct disease prevention campaigns and provide primary healthcare in remote areas (Jennings 2008; _Northern Rhodesia Medical Report_ 1934; Pringle 2019). In Northern Rhodesia, the trust the missionaries established led to increased visits to missionary healthcare facilities, and the expansion of their services during the colonial period allowed them to address the growing medical needs of the African population (Gelfand 1961; Rotberg 1965). The financial support from the colonial administration and donations from private entities and churches back home played a significant role in facilitating the expansion of missionary medical services (Gelfand 1961; Rotberg 1965). Outpatients refer to those seeking medical care at either missionary or state hospitals without staying overnight. At the same time, inpatients stayed at a hospital overnight or longer for treatment.

## **Africans and the diffusion of healthcare** 

The contributions of Africans in establishing and accepting Western medicine in Africa have often been overlooked in early medical literature, which tends to emphasise the efforts of European missionaries (Crozier 2007; Gelfand 1961; Hardiman 2006; Rotberg 1965; Vaughan 1991, 1994). African involvement in spreading and contextualising Western medicine cannot be ignored (Kalusa 2007; Pringle 2019). However, the role of African medical staff in adopting and disseminating Western medical practices remains relatively unexplored in the existing literature (Greenwood 2015; Iliffe 1998; Kalusa 2007; Vaughan 1991). Therefore, the fundamental impetus of this section is to highlight the importance of African auxiliaries in the spread of Western medical praxis. 

When missionaries arrived in Africa, they faced numerous challenges, including the land’s vastness, limited numbers, susceptibility to tropical diseases, and language barriers with the indigenous people (Gelfand 1961). They soon realised the necessity of involving Africans in their evangelistic efforts to reach a larger population. This approach was advantageous as Africans possessed better knowledge of the land and its terrains, spoke local languages, were cost-effective to maintain, and demonstrated resilience against tropical diseases (Gelfand 1961; Rotberg 1965). Furthermore Protestant missionaries aimed to transfer the responsibility of mission work to indigenous churches, enabling the rapid spread of the Gospel (Gann 1968). 

The colonial government entrusted missionaries with the task of training African medical staff (Gelfand 1961). Before establishing medical training institutions, African medical officers were trained at missionary hospital sites. Dr Walter Fisher of the Plymouth Brethren played a dominant role in training African medical staff in Northern Rhodesia, focusing on elementary physiology, anatomy, and entomology (Kalusa 2007). Dr Fisher believed that equipping Africans with Western medical knowledge would liberate them from attributing all sickness to witchcraft (ibid). The notion that Africans attributed all 

AFRICAN STUDIES 13 

illnesses to witchcraft and sorcery was prevalent in Western medical discourse (Waite 1992). Anthropological evidence suggests that even before Europeans discovered microbiology, Africans possessed knowledge of various illnesses and their origins (Waite 1992). In fact, Africans employed traditional medical practices to treat illnesses. 

The training African medical auxiliaries received at missionary hospitals did not eradicate their prior etiological beliefs (Kalusa 2007). Instead, African auxiliaries reinterpreted acquired Western medical knowledge within an African context. During a visit to Kalene Hospital, Dr H.S. de Boer observed that a lack of deep understanding of medical knowledge, a limited command of the English language, and the absence of vernacular terms for certain medical terminologies hindered Africans from effectively translating complex medical concepts into local languages (ibid). Consequently, African auxiliaries administered Western biomedicine through indigenous cosmogonies and healing perspectives (Kalusa 2007). Missionaries recognised that African medical trainees would not completely abandon their pre-existing medical practices (Kalusa 2014a). The reinterpretation of Western medical knowledge using pagan medical idioms facilitated the acceptance of missionary medicine among Africans (Kalusa 2014b). 

A medical committee was formed during the Missionary Conference held in Broken Hill in 1931 to foster cooperation among missionaries and prevent duplication of medical work ( _Northern Rhodesia Medical Report 1931_ ). Initially, missionaries primarily trained male nurses, as medical work was considered unsuitable for female Africans. In fact, the 1927 Missionary General Conference concluded that the medical staff should consist of capable and responsible African males (Kumwenda 2005). 

The colonial government provided grants to missionaries for training African medical staff and delivering medical services to the local population. In 1931, the British colonial government emphasised the need to train indigenous sanitary personnel, responsible for touring villages, promoting improved sanitation practices, and disseminating knowledge on preventative medicine ( _Northern Rhodesia Medical Report 1931_ ). That same year, Dr Chisholm of the Livingstonia Mission in the Isoka district trained six African sanitary orderlies. Before establishing formal medical training schools, missionaries trained nurses and orderlies at their hospitals. The 1932 medical report indicates that many missionary societies operating health centres in the territory employed trained nurses at their stations. In 1936, the African Medical Training School was established to primarily train African male nurses to treat various diseases and injuries (Kumwenda 2005). 

In various African regions, missionaries were solely responsible for training African medical workers or collaborated with the colonial government. In Uganda, missionaries played a joint role in establishing the Maternity Training School to train African teenage girls from different regions in Uganda for maternity care (Summers 1991). Similarly, medical training programmes in East Africa and Uganda, which trained African medical assistants, were missionary initiated (Iliffe 1998). In the case of Malawi, despite the government opening a medical training school, many Africans in the healthcare sector received exclusive training from missionaries (Hokkanen 2019). In fact, during the early colonial period in Malawi, missionaries implemented some of the most ambitious medical training programmes in Anglophone Africa, preparing African medical staff to work beyond the protectorate (Hokkanen 2019). Therefore, trained African medical workers played a crucial role in establishing and expanding the public healthcare system in Africa.

For instance, in Malawi, many African medical assistants, primarily men, were referred to as ‘dispensary boys’ despite performing substantial nursing and dispensing duties (Vaughan 1991). However, in East Africa, despite their training and experience, colonial society still recognised Africans as assistants (Iliffe 1998). 

## **State, missionary and African auxiliary effort in fighting epidemics** 

During the British South African Company’s administration of Northern Rhodesia, their primary focus was on disease prevention rather than curative medical care (Gelfand 1961). The prevention of disease among the local population was of utmost concern to the White settler population, as an outbreak of disease would also impact them (ibid). Similarly, in Kenya, the British colonial government’s main goal within the Medical Department was not solely to cater to the health needs of the African population but rather to safeguard the European population and ensure that epidemic outbreaks did not disrupt the colonial economy (Dawson 1987). Upon assuming control of Northern Rhodesia from the Company in 1924, the British colonial government continued to prioritise disease prevention while allocating more resources for developing and improving African hospitals. 

Furthermore, the missionaries and trained Africans played crucial roles as agents in the fight against disease in Northern Rhodesia. Although the colonial government possessed 

16 M. C. CHISENI 

the necessary technologies to combat disease, the successful vaccination and outreach to the African population would not have been possible without the cooperation and coordinated efforts of the African staff and missionaries. The Chief Medical Officer consistently emphasised the significance of missionaries and Africans in implementing colonial medical campaigns, particularly in rural areas ( _Northern Rhodesia Medical Report 1932_ ). 

This section of the article focuses on comprehending the nature of colonial responses to epidemic outbreaks and highlighting the contributions of missionaries and Africans in supporting the colonial administration’s medical campaigns. 

## _**Smallpox**_ 

In 1900, the first smallpox outbreak occurred in Northern Rhodesia, believed to have spread from the Zambezi Valley through the Tete trade route (Gelfand 1961). Subsequent smallpox outbreaks occurred in 1929, 1945, 1955, and 1963 (Fenner, Henderson, Arita et al. 1988). Figure 10 presents the number of smallpox cases in Zambia, Malawi, and Zimbabwe from 1920 to 1966, indicating similar breakout histories among the three countries. Specifically, Zambia experienced variola major outbreaks in 1929 and variola minor outbreaks in 1943/45, 1955, and 1963. The British colonial government, with the assistance of trained Africans and missionaries, intensified lymph vaccination campaigns in the affected regions. Mass vaccination campaigns colonial governments implemented in other parts of Africa, with African personnel largely conducting, yielded effective results (Schneider 2009). 

The early work of missionaries among the African population established a strong rapport between them. Consequently, the colonial government capitalised on this camaraderie to reach the African population during vaccination campaigns (Gann 1969).

Western medicine faced varying degrees of acceptance across African societies. Although vaccination efforts against smallpox were successful in Kenya, they were often carried out forcefully (Crozier 2007). The virulence of the plague was such that people succumbed to the infection within two days (Gelfand 1961). To contain the rapid spread of infection, the administration of North-Eastern Rhodesia implemented measures that included a campaign to exterminate rats in the affected villages between February 1917 and April 1918. Approximately 50 000 rats were estimated to have been killed as part of these efforts. 

Various measures were implemented in response to the bubonic plague outbreak in North-Eastern Rhodesia. These measures included the closure of the Mzimba-Rhodesian and Nyasaland-Northern Rhodesia borders, restricted movement of Africans in affected areas, and the burning down of some infected villages (Gelfand 1961). Notably, the anti-rat campaign was identified as the most effective measure in reducing and ending the plague, with approximately £300 per month allocated for this campaign (Gelfand 1961). Similar measures were implemented in Nyasaland to address a concurrent plague outbreak (Hokkanen 2019). Christian missionaries played a vital role in combating bubonic plagues, seizing the opportunity to provide medical care while preaching the gospel to Africans (Samundengu 2011). The British South African Company had invested minimal resources in developing medical care for Africans, leaving missionaries to fill this crucial gap. Furthermore, in districts like Mwinilunga and Kasempa, the District Officer delegated medical responsibilities to untrained Africans, who implemented various health measures to control the plague’s spread among the African population. Thus, both missionaries and these untrained Africans proved invaluable to the British South African Company in combating the plague throughout the region. 

Following the bubonic plague, the world faced the devastating Spanish Flu pandemic, which occurred from February 1918 to April 1920 and affected an estimated 500 million people worldwide (Trilla, Trilla & Daer 2008). The death toll from the Spanish Flu is believed to have ranged between 20 to 50 million (Trilla et al. 2008). The first flu outbreak in Africa was reported in Sierra Leone in August 1918, likely facilitated by the movement of African soldiers recruited during World War I (Tomkins 1994). Nyasaland and Zanzibar initiated screening measures for travellers passing through their territories, quarantining those suspected of carrying the virus (Musambachime, 1998; Tomkins 1994). However, Northern Rhodesia delayed implementing screening measures, potentially resulting in the troops returning from the war in early October 1918 introducing the flu (Musambachime 1993). It is suggested that the limited response in British colonies during the pandemic may have been due to the Colonial Offices’ focus primarily on tropical diseases (Tomkins 1994). 

The Spanish Flu had a varied impact in Northern Rhodesia, with the virus spreading extensively along the rail line and urban areas experiencing higher mortality rates compared to rural regions (Musambachime 1993). Specifically, the eastern areas of the Luangwa Valley, the Luapula River, the shores of Lake Mweru, and the Southern, Central and Northwestern regions of the territory recorded significant mortality rates (Musambachime 1993). Pneumonic complications accompanied the flu, contributing to the high death rates, particularly among young men aged fifteen to 40 (Musambachime 1993). 

In response, the Northern Rhodesian administrators established temporary isolation centres known as _mitandas_ (Musambachime 1998). These _mitandas_ were located away from villages, and only a few relatives were permitted to visit patients (Musambachime 1998). Villagers were encouraged to temporarily relocate, with the belief that dispersing 

AFRICAN STUDIES 21 

the population would help reduce the spread of the disease. Additional measures to contain the flu included border closures, restrictions on large gatherings, school closures, discouragement of missionary tours, and, in some areas, a complete prohibition on such tours. The British South African Company suspended the recruitment of African migrant labour, and mine operations were significantly scaled down (Musambachime 1998).[3] 

Treatment in the region primarily relied on the South African Institute of Medical Research’s pneumococcal vaccine. It was noted that by September 1919, there were only mild recurrences of the flu in most parts of Northern Rhodesia (Gelfand 1961). However, in 1935, it was determined that the principal cause of sporadic flu and subsequent pneumonia outbreaks was Streptococcus pyogenes. To address this, a locally developed vaccine using recovered organisms was introduced, proving to be more cost-effective than the previously obtained vaccine from the South African Institute of Medical Research. The development of this local vaccine enabled the colonial government to expand its vaccination campaigns in the territory, significantly reducing influenza infections ( _Northern Rhodesia Medical Report_ 1935, 1939, 1945, 1946). The missionaries and African auxiliaries played a crucial role in combating influenza, particularly in rural areas (Hokkanen 2019). A highlight is an incident in 1918, during the peak of the flu pandemic, where colonial medical officers sent influenza serum to missionaries in Chitambo, one of the affected areas. Medical missionaries were important as the colonial medical system was limited during the flu outbreak (Musambachime 1998). Their cooperation with missionaries was crucial, as missionaries could provide medical relief to the general population who were beyond the reach of hospitals and dispensaries. At each major mission station, various denominations had at least one qualified doctor trained in tropical medicine (Musambachime 1998). A missionary with basic medical training handled medical cases if a doctor was unavailable. In addition, African assistants received medical training from the doctors and were deployed at hospitals and outstations. 

In concluding this section, it is crucial to highlight that while the colonial government responded to disease breakouts, colonial medical interventions have faced criticism, particularly regarding certain diseases. Some colonial medical responses exhibited racial segregation, undermining even well-intentioned public health programmes (Molefi 2001). During the 1930s, a bubonic plague outbreak occurred in Southern Africa, believed to have spread through the movement of migrant workers in the region. In an attempt to curb the infection’s spread, Northern Rhodesian authorities quarantined all migrant workers and African passengers travelling from Bechuanaland for twelve days in 1937. However, European travellers were not subjected to similar measures and were only subjected to medical examinations at their destinations rather than at the port of entry. Furthermore, colonial interventions disrupted the daily lives of Africans, including forced rat hole filling, compulsory disinfection campaigns compromising home privacy, and prohibitions on consuming certain animals (Mushingeh 1984). 

These repressive anti-plague campaigns were predominantly imposed on Africans residing in urban areas and mining townships in Northern Rhodesia (Gelfand 1961). Compared to the European population, African miners and their families in the Copperbelt region were coerced and faced hostility as part of malaria control, which highlights the discriminatory nature of colonial campaigns in urban areas (Schumaker 2011). The presence of mosquito habitats in wetland areas, where African populations cultivated 

22 M. C. CHISENI 

vegetable gardens, posed a challenge, as controlling mosquito populations would require destroying these gardens. Additionally, anti-malaria spraying campaigns subjected Africans to more invasive measures than Europeans. While European homes were sprayed from the outside, the colonial government also sprayed the interior of African miners’ homes with Dichlorodiphenyltrichloroethane (DDT), raising concerns about health implications, especially for vulnerable people (Schumaker 2011). 

Although discriminatory intervention practices were prevalent in British colonial Africa, British colonial governments employed more advanced methods to control disease breakouts in urban areas compared to the French (Echenberg 2002). This difference can be attributed to the British investment in establishing schools of public health during the Sanitary Reform Movement of the 1870s, while the French made limited investments in public health and lacked institutions for training public health practitioners. Consequently, French colonial governments were less prepared to handle disease outbreaks, resorting to extreme measures such as expelling African populations and burning houses in response to outbreaks in Senegal. In contrast, the British had already implemented DDT-based control measures for urban plagues in the early twentieth century, which proved more efficient than burning African houses. It was only in the mid-twentieth century that the French controlled the disease with DDT (Echenberg 2002). 

## **Conclusion** 

The primary objective of this study was to examine the development of Western healthcare during the colonial period and assess the respective contributions of missionaries, the state, and African auxiliaries to healthcare outreach among the African population, encompassing both curative and preventive aspects. To achieve this, newly collected data from Northern Rhodesian medical reports on hospital establishments and outpatient attendances were utilised to analyse the spatial distribution of missionary hospitals and African colonial hospitals, thus comparing the extent of their outreach. The annual medical reports also provided insights into the colonial government measures during outbreaks. 

The spatial distribution analysis revealed that African colonial hospitals were predominantly concentrated in twelve locations along the rail line and main administrative centres throughout the colonial period. In contrast, missionary hospitals were primarily situated in rural areas where most African population resided. By 1953, the missionary societies had established 89 hospitals in the territory. In Northern Rhodesia, the state and missionaries collaborated to provide medical services to the local populace, with clearly defined roles and a shared understanding that the state alone could not meet the curative demands of the rural population, relying on missionaries to fulfil that role. 

Furthermore, missionaries played a crucial role in training Africans in basic medical procedures to serve in their medical stations and colonial state hospitals. Both the state and missionaries relied heavily on these trained African healthcare workers to expand the reach of medical services among the indigenous population. As highlighted in this study, the trained Africans, referred to as ‘medics’, adapted and interpreted the medical knowledge the missionaries imparted to align with pre-existing indigenous medical practices, thus playing a significant role in expanding and disseminating missionary medicine. The coordinated efforts between missionaries and trained African staff also proved 

AFRICAN STUDIES 23 

successful in combating various epidemic outbreaks in the territory. However, it is important to acknowledge that colonial medical interventions sometimes exhibited racial segregation, particularly in urban areas. 

In conclusion, while it is widely recognised that missionaries pioneered Western healthcare in Northern Rhodesia, limited knowledge existed regarding the extent of their medical outreach, their collaboration with indigenous medical workers, and their contributions to fighting epidemics. This study provides a quantitative perspective on the scope of missionary medical outreach among the local population. By employing a quantitative methodology, this research offers new data that can facilitate cross-comparisons with similar studies conducted in other regions. Moreover, this study challenges the prevailing narrative that primarily glorified the role of white doctors, shedding light on the importance of African agency in the dissemination of Western medicine. 

Additionally, this study addresses a longstanding gap in the African medical missionary literature by providing quantitative evidence to substantiate the significant impact of medical missionaries. While similar data may exist for other African countries, studies of this nature have not yet been conducted, underscoring the pioneering nature of this research. As a result, this study advances the frontiers of African medical research and encourages further investigations of a similar nature in other African countries. Furthermore, the availability of data through this study facilitates long-term analyses to trace the evolution and development of healthcare in Zambia. Future research endeavours can explore questions such as the persistence of colonial medical infrastructure and its implications for contemporary healthcare development in Africa. A deeper understanding of health inequalities in Africa can be achieved by comprehending healthcare in a historical context. Linking contemporary healthcare disparities to the level of colonial health investments becomes possible when similar studies are conducted across African countries, thereby opening new avenues of research.
