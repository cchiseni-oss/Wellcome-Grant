# Research Vision (revised)

*The Long Shadow of Mission Medicine: Colonial Missionary Healthcare, Institutional Trust, and the Spatial Burden of Disease in Sub-Saharan Africa*

## The problem

In much of sub-Saharan Africa a puzzle remains unresolved in health systems. Across large areas, formal health facilities exist and are within physical reach, yet utilisation, particularly of preventive, antenatal, and child health services, remains low, and disease burden stays high and spatially uneven (Weiss et al. 2019; Local Burden of Disease Collaborators 2020). The standard explanations are supply-side and contemporary: poverty, distance, the density and quality of facilities, the availability of staff and drugs (Peters et al. 2008; Ensor and Cooper 2004). These matter, but a substantial residual remains: communities that look similar on income, education, and distance to the nearest clinic still differ markedly in how readily they engage with formal health systems, even after accounting for these supply-side factors (Ensor and Cooper 2004). My central claim is that part of this residual is historical, and that it runs through institutional trust, the degree to which a community treats the formal health system as a legitimate, reliable institution worth engaging with.

Trust is now recognised as a foundational determinant of health-system performance. Gilson argues that trust is constitutive of health care as a social institution, shaping whether people present early, disclose honestly, accept advice, and return (Gilson 2003, 2005). Where institutional trust is low, people delay care, bypass nearer facilities, default on treatment, and rely on informal providers, with measurable consequences for the control of infectious and chronic disease (Østergaard 2015; Arakelyan et al. 2021). But the literature treats trust as essentially contemporary, a product of present-day experiences with the health system. It lacks a historical account of why institutional trust varies so sharply across space. That is the gap this project fills.

## The historical argument

In British colonial Africa, the formal healthcare a community first encountered was mainly missionary. From the late nineteenth century, Christian missionary societies were the dominant providers of hospitals, dispensaries, and trained medical staff, long before many colonial states built meaningful public health systems (Vaughan 1991; Hokkanen 2019; Doyle, Meier zu Selhausen and Weisdorf 2020). Medical work was central to the missionary enterprise: by the early twentieth century Protestant medical missions alone were treating millions of patients a year (Calvi and Mantovanelli 2018). The encounter was uneven and consequential. In some places missionary medicine was experienced as care, building familiarity and a durable disposition to seek formal treatment (Jennings 2008); in others it was entangled with coercion, social control, and the disruption of existing healing systems, leaving suspicion that could outlast the missions themselves (Vaughan 1991; Crozier 2007).

My argument is that this colonial geography of missionary medicine laid down an enduring substrate of institutional trust, positive in some places and negative in others, and that this substrate still shapes health-seeking behaviour and, through it, the spatial distribution of disease burden today. The economic-history literature has shown that colonial-era institutions leave deep and persistent imprints: on education (Wantchekon et al. 2015; Nunn 2014), on human capital and culture (Valencia Caicedo 2019), on trust in the wake of the slave trade (Nunn and Wantchekon 2011), and, closest to my own work, on attitudes to medicine, where Lowes and Montero show that the violent colonial medical campaigns of central Africa left a persistent legacy of distrust in medicine and lower vaccination uptake (Lowes and Montero 2021). On the other side of the ledger, Calvi and Mantovanelli show that proximity to a Protestant medical mission in colonial India is associated with better health outcomes today, working through durable changes in health habits and engagement rather than surviving infrastructure or conversion (Calvi and Mantovanelli 2018). These findings frame the stakes: colonial medicine could leave either a trust dividend or a trust deficit. No study has measured this legacy at scale across British colonial Africa, identified missionary medicine specifically as its source, or tested institutional trust as the behavioural channel through which it still shapes disease burden.

## Aims and research questions

**RQ1 (the historical map).** Where was colonial missionary health infrastructure located across British colonial Africa, and can it be reconstructed at facility level, individual hospitals and dispensaries rather than the coarse mission-station points the literature relies on, including variation by denomination, territory, and period?

**RQ2 (trust, behaviour, and burden).** Do communities historically exposed to missionary health infrastructure show systematically different institutional trust and health-seeking behaviour today than comparable communities that were not, and does that difference, through behaviour, predict a measurable, spatially patterned disease-burden penalty?

**RQ3 (mechanisms).** Through what channels is this inheritance transmitted? Candidates include the survival of faith-based facilities as mediators of the community and state health relationship; the intergenerational transmission of health-worker norms; the embedding of health-seeking as a social and religious norm; and, conversely, the legacy of coercive colonial medical campaigns experienced as social control.

**RQ4 (community meaning).** How do people in communities with different colonial health histories understand their own relationship with formal healthcare? This centres the values, memory, and priorities of communities rarely asked to explain their health-seeking behaviour in historical terms.

## Why this is bold

The project reframes a problem the health-systems field treats as contemporary and supply-side as one that is also historical and demand-side. The economic-history literature has shown that colonial institutions leave persistent imprints, but it has measured outcomes (schooling, income, height, HIV, vaccination) rather than the behavioural pathway of engagement with health systems, and it has not tested missionary medicine at scale across British colonial Africa as a determinant of contemporary health-seeking. No study has joined these fields: linking the colonial geography of missionary medicine to present-day trust and health-seeking, and then asking the harder question, whether that trust gradient is itself the channel through which colonial history still shapes the spatial distribution of disease burden. If it is, it changes how we explain a major component of health inequality on the continent, and identifies a lever, trust, that infrastructure spending alone will not move.

## Why this is creative

The creativity lies in the combination and in the source material. I integrate four bodies of evidence that have never been brought together: (i) an original, facility-level historical map of mission medicine; (ii) georeferenced contemporary survey data on behaviour and trust; (iii) high-resolution spatial disease-burden estimates; and (iv) community testimony from qualitative fieldwork. The analytical move that ties them together is a formal mediation design: rather than merely correlating historical exposure with present outcomes, I decompose the effect of historical mission exposure on disease burden into the share running through institutional trust, the share through health-seeking behaviour, and any residual direct effect (for example, surviving infrastructure or workforce). This turns a plausible historical story into a testable causal pathway. The qualitative component is not decoration: it identifies which mechanisms operate and lets communities articulate meanings no survey item captures, including negative legacies that a naive "missions were good for health" reading would miss.

A second source of creativity is the historical data itself, where my approach departs deliberately from the literature. Almost all quantitative work on African missions locates missions using digitised missionary atlases (Beach 1903; Roome 1925; Streit), as in Cagé and Rueda (2016) and the recent Stellenbosch work of Brown on Bible translation, which digitises four Protestant and two Catholic atlases (Brown 2025). These atlases are invaluable but demonstrably incomplete: Jedwab, Meier zu Selhausen and Moradi show, comparing atlases against the ecclesiastical census returns in colonial Blue Books, that they omit between 76% and 98% of missions, over-representing prominent European-staffed "residence" stations and missing the hinterland, biasing estimates of mission effects (Jedwab, Meier zu Selhausen and Moradi 2022). My dataset is built from the source family they identify as the corrective, and from two complementary streams within it: the ecclesiastical returns in the Blue Books, which log missionary station provision society by society and territory by territory, and the Colonial Medical Reports, which record subsidised mission (and government) hospitals and dispensaries with their station-level, annual patient volumes. I have already done this in my PhD, geo-referencing missionary stations from the ecclesiastical returns (1924 to 1948) and mission hospitals from the medical reports (1933 to 1953) for Zambia. Scaling this administrative-record method across British colonial Africa will produce a dataset more comprehensive and more accurate for facility locations than any atlas-based source, a contribution to the field independent of my own analysis.

## Why this is high quality: approach and workplan

The programme runs over 48 months in four work packages. The empirical scope is British colonial Africa, where the administrative sources (the Blue Books and the Colonial Medical Reports) form a single, comparable series, and contemporary outcomes are analysed across the former British colonies, where survey coverage is strongest. Holding to one colonial administrative system keeps the treatment, placebo, and controls like-for-like and avoids the source heterogeneity that other colonial records would introduce. A research assistant will support digitisation and geocoding under my supervision.

**WP1: The dataset (months 1 to 18).** I will build a geo-referenced dataset of colonial missionary stations and the medical facilities among them from two complementary sources. The ecclesiastical returns in the Blue Books give the full station universe (by society), including non-medical stations. The Colonial Medical Reports record subsidised mission hospitals and dispensaries, their society, and their station-level annual patient volumes (in-patients, and out-patient cases and attendances). Joining the two lets me flag which stations operated a medical facility, distinguish a hospital (in-patient capacity) from a dispensary (out-patient only) directly from the returns, and measure the intensity and duration of medical activity rather than mere presence. Each record carries society, facility type, coordinates, years of activity, patient volumes where available, and provenance. Sources are held at the British Library, the LSE Library (Colonial Records, which holds the medical reports), the National Archives (Kew), SOAS, and the Bodleian Libraries (Rhodes House), supplemented by the British Online Archives. My Zambian dataset provides the validated coding protocol and benchmark. The dataset will be validated against existing point sources (Cagé and Rueda's atlas data; the Mapping Missions data, Hedde-von Westernhagen and Becker 2022; and Brown's digitised atlas data), explicitly quantifying the atlas-omission problem (Jedwab, Meier zu Selhausen and Moradi 2022), and released open access.

**WP2: Trust, behaviour, and burden (months 12 to 30).** I will link historical exposure to contemporary georeferenced data: individual health-seeking and care-experience measures from the DHS (cluster-georeferenced utilisation, antenatal care, vaccination, care-seeking, place of delivery) and Afrobarometer (Round 9 covers 53,444 respondents across 39 countries, on healthcare access and experience); faith-based facility ownership from the KEMRI-Wellcome and WHO facility database (Maina et al. 2019; 98,745 facilities, 50 countries) and the WHO AFRO Geodatabase, whose faith-based flag also identifies likely descendants of former mission hospitals (a key mediator); and high-resolution disease-burden estimates from the IHME Local Burden of Disease project (Local Burden of Disease Collaborators 2020; Weiss et al. 2019). I will model what each source actually measures: Afrobarometer captures access and experience rather than an explicit "trust in health facilities" item, and IHME provides prevalence and mortality at 5 by 5 km but DALYs at administrative level.

**WP3: Identification and mechanisms (months 24 to 42).** The design follows and extends the credible-identification strategies established for this literature. I combine matching on observable determinants of mission location with an instrumental-variable strategy exploiting differences across societies in their propensity to undertake medical (as opposed to purely evangelical or educational) work, the approach of Calvi and Mantovanelli (2018) and Cagé and Rueda (2016), which I have applied for Zambia. By joining the station universe (ecclesiastical returns) to the medical facilities (medical reports), I distinguish medical missions (with a hospital or dispensary) from missions without health facilities, using the latter as a placebo to isolate the health-specific channel. Because mission placement was endogenous to healthier, more accessible, richer locations (Jedwab, Meier zu Selhausen and Moradi 2022), I control directly for the determinants they identify (historical malaria, railways, cash-crop suitability, urban access). I also control for historical state health provision using British colonial government-hospital data obtained from Bolt and Cilliers (2025), separating the missionary effect from where the colonial state invested. I then run the mediation analysis, decomposing the historical-exposure effect on disease burden into trust and behavioural channels versus a residual direct effect, and test the persistence of faith-based facility ownership as a mediating pathway (faith-based organisations remain major providers today; Olivier et al. 2015), drawing on evidence that African medical auxiliaries reinterpreted and carried forward missionary medicine within local contexts (Kalusa 2007). Identification is probed with placebo future-mission tests, distance-based falsification, sensitivity analysis for unobserved confounding (Oster bounds), and robustness to alternative exposure definitions and to DHS cluster-coordinate displacement.

**WP4: Community meaning (months 20 to 32).** I will conduct qualitative fieldwork in four purposively selected Zambian communities, two historically served by missionary hospitals and two comparable communities without such exposure, building on the primary fieldwork I led during my Gothenburg postdoc. Semi-structured interviews and sex-disaggregated focus groups explore how colonial health histories are remembered, transmitted, and expressed, identifying which mechanisms operate and surfacing community-articulated meanings, including negative legacies, that the quantitative data cannot capture. Communities will help interpret and validate findings and shape the policy outputs.

## Feasibility and risk

The programme is ambitious but feasible because its hardest component, the archival construction of a geo-referenced missionary health dataset, is precisely the work I have already done and published for Zambia; the methods (archival reconstruction, geocoding, matching and IV identification, mixed-methods fieldwork) are established in my track record. To keep construction across British colonial Africa within 48 months, a research assistant supports digitisation and geocoding, which also strengthens the capacity-building dimension of the award. The design guarantees a core deliverable: headline results are produced on the best-covered territories first, with wider coverage pursued thereafter, so the central findings do not depend on complete coverage. That multi-territory digitisation from these exact sources is tractable is demonstrated by Bolt and Cilliers (2025), who built a comparable state-healthcare dataset for twelve British African colonies from the same Medical Reports and Blue Books.

The principal risks are handled directly. Archival completeness varies by territory: I phase coverage (richest sources first), record provenance and confidence for every facility, triangulate government, denominational, and medical-survey sources, and publish full documentation of gaps. Mission-placement endogeneity is addressed by the controls and identification strategies of Jedwab, Meier zu Selhausen and Moradi (2022), Calvi and Mantovanelli (2018), and Cagé and Rueda (2016), with placebo and falsification tests and sensitivity bounds. Spatial linkage under deliberate DHS displacement is handled with established methods (buffer zones, distance bands, sensitivity to displacement radius). For ecological inference, I retain individual-level estimation where possible (DHS, Afrobarometer) and avoid over-claiming at the individual level from grid-cell burden data.

## Key stages, location, and milestones

I will be hosted at Stellenbosch University (Laboratory for the Economics of Africa's Past), with archival work in the United Kingdom and fieldwork in Zambia.

- **Phase 1, dataset construction (months 1 to 18), UK archives:** archival research at the British Library, SOAS, the National Archives (Kew), the LSE Library (Colonial Records), and the Bodleian Libraries, with research-assistant-supported coding and geocoding, and validation against the Zambian benchmark. *Milestone: v1 dataset for British colonial Africa and draft data paper (month 18).*
- **Phase 2, quantitative analysis (months 12 to 30), Stellenbosch:** linkage of DHS, Afrobarometer, facility, and IHME data to historical exposure; main and mediation models. *Milestone: first empirical article submitted (month 30).*
- **Phase 3, qualitative fieldwork (months 20 to 32), Zambia:** four community case studies with interviews and focus groups, and community validation (ethics approvals initiated in month 1). *Milestone: fieldwork complete and coded (month 32).*
- **Phase 4, mechanisms and synthesis (months 30 to 42), Stellenbosch:** mixed-methods integration and mediation decomposition. *Milestone: disease-burden and mediation article submitted (month 42).*
- **Phase 5, dissemination and capacity (months 42 to 48):** open dataset release and policy briefs co-produced with communities and a public-health partner. *Milestone: dataset publicly released and policy briefs published (month 48).*

## Background and the work leading to this project

This project is the direct extension of my published research. My doctoral work at Lund University (*Here I Am, Send Me*, 2022; open-access monograph, Springer 2024) established that the historical footprint of Christian missions in Zambia can be measured precisely and linked to long-run outcomes, from an original geo-referenced dataset of missionary health stations in Northern Rhodesia. Two peer-reviewed contributions prove the two halves of this proposal. In *African Studies* (Chiseni 2025) I show how missionary colonial healthcare can be reconstructed at fine spatial and temporal resolution from archival sources, the method I scale here. In *Economic History of Developing Regions* (Chiseni 2024) I demonstrate a credible identification strategy linking colonial missionary exposure to a contemporary health outcome (HIV and sexual behaviour), establishing that the long-run causal approach works for health behaviour, not only education or income. I have also led six months of primary qualitative fieldwork in rural Zambia, so the community-facing component rests on demonstrated capacity.

## Expected outcomes

By the end of the award I will have delivered: (1) the first openly available, facility-level dataset of colonial missionary health infrastructure across British colonial Africa, more complete than atlas-based sources and an enabling resource for the field; (2) the first large-scale evidence on whether colonial missionary medicine shaped contemporary institutional trust and, through it, disease burden; (3) a demand-side, historically grounded contribution to health-systems research; (4) community-centred qualitative evidence on how health histories are lived and remembered; and (5) policy-relevant findings on where low utilisation reflects a historically rooted trust deficit rather than poverty or distance, with implications for how health communication, community-health-worker programmes, and facility governance are designed. The programme also establishes me as an independent leader bridging economic history, health-systems research, and spatial epidemiology.
